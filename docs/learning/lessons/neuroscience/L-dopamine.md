# Lesson object — Dopamine: the "wanting" chemical (not the pleasure one)
_This file is the **meaning** of the lesson, separate from how it's shown. Text now,
VR later, same meaning. Learner-agnostic: a child's version would be a different
object, not a re-skin of this one._

- **Concept:** Dopamine is famous as the "pleasure chemical," and that's wrong. It's
  the brain's **wanting / pursuit** signal — it spikes when a reward is *anticipated*,
  to push you to go get it (motivation, focus, effort), more than while you actually
  enjoy the thing. It tags what's "worth chasing." The *liking* — the pleasure of
  having it — runs largely on a **different** system (opioids/endorphins). So dopamine
  is about the chase, not the catch.
- **Why it matters:** Almost every "focus / drive / motivation" booster (stimulants,
  and a lot of hyped nootropics) is sold on "raises dopamine." If you think dopamine =
  pleasure, you'll misread what they do. Seeing dopamine as *wanting* explains both why
  those compounds make you pursue harder, and why easy dopamine hits (phones, junk,
  doom-scroll) can quietly **kill** your motivation for slow, hard rewards.
- **Prerequisites:** [[L-adenosine]] (a neurotransmitter is a signal, and its real job
  is often misread — caffeine *blocks* rather than *boosts*).
- **Mastery (observable):** The learner can explain, **unprompted and in their own
  words**, that dopamine is mainly about **wanting/pursuit/motivation** (the drive to
  get a reward), not the pleasure of having it. Bonus: they connect that to why
  spiking dopamine on easy rewards can sap drive for harder, slower goals.
- **Hint (don't reveal — let them derive it):** "Everyone calls dopamine the 'pleasure
  chemical.' But picture the exact moment it spikes hardest — is it bigger when you're
  *about to get* something you want, or *while you're enjoying* it? And if it's really
  about the chase… what is dopamine *for* — feeling good, or making you go get it?"
- **Worked example:** _(optional slot — skipped for this adult, derive-with-hints
  learner.)_
- **Misconceptions to surface:** (1) "Dopamine = pleasure" — no; it's wanting/pursuit,
  and *liking* is a separate system. (2) "More dopamine = happier" — no; floods of easy
  dopamine can blunt the system, so the slow rewards (study, training) stop feeling
  worth it.

---
## Render — TEXT (now)
Open with the due retrieval rep, then the Hint question; let them predict whether
dopamine fires on the chase or the enjoyment. Confirm wanting ≠ liking, then connect it
to their own goal (focus boosters "raise dopamine" = raise *drive*), and surface the two
misconceptions. Plain words, no jargon dump.

## Render — VR SCENE (later — meaning unchanged, only the display differs)
> A glowing reward sits across a field. As you start moving toward it, a meter labelled
> **DOPAMINE** climbs — highest while you're *chasing*. The instant you grab the reward,
> the meter *drops*, and a second, quieter meter — **LIKING** — briefly lights. Replay
> with an "easy reward" you can grab over and over with no effort: the dopamine meter
> spikes fast each time, then sags lower and lower — and the field's faraway, harder
> reward visibly dims, as if it stopped being worth the walk.
