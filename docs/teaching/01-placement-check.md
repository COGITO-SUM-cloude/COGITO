# Starting-Line Check — Reading + Math

_A calm, ~15-minute check to find the learner's **real** level, so the practice we
build lands exactly where he is. This is **not a test or a grade** — it's finding
the starting line. Keep it light. If he gets tired or frustrated, **stop** — where
he stopped is itself the answer we need._

> For the older brother running it: sit beside him, not across. Say "let's find
> where to start — there are no wrong answers, just a starting point." Praise effort
> ("nice try sounding that out"), never correctness. Don't make him finish a section
> that's clearly too hard — note it and move on. The whole thing is information *for
> us*, not a judgment *of him*.

---

## How to mark each item
For every item, jot one of:
- **✓** = got it, smooth
- **~** = got it but slow / needed a second try
- **✗** = couldn't, or guessed
- **stop** = where you stopped a section

You can write right on this page or on scrap paper. Then type the results back to
Cogito (roughly is fine — "letter sounds all good; CVC got cat/dog/sun, stuck on
bed/pig; grade-1 list got about half").

# PART A — READING  (do this part carefully; reading is the priority)

## A1. Letter sounds — say the **sound**, not the letter name
(e.g. for `m` he says "mmm", for `a` he says "a" as in *apple*)

`m   s   a   t   p   f   i   n   d   o   b   c   g   h   e   r   l   u`

→ note which sounds he knows / mixes up: ____________________

## A2. Sound out these simple words (short, 3-letter)
`cat   dog   sun   bed   pig   run   top   leg   mud   fan`

→ how many of 10: ____   which ones tripped him: ____________________

## A3. Trickier words (two letters making one sound, and blends)
`ship   chop   that   when   stop   flag   drum   lunch   brush   stamp`

→ how many of 10: ____   notes: ____________________

## A4. Word lists by grade — read across; **stop a list when he misses about 3**
- **Grade 1:** `the   and   look   play   big   jump   said   like   went   here`
- **Grade 2:** `because   found   around   every   three   water   before   would   animal   morning`
- **Grade 3:** `through   thought   different   important   enough   special   believe   sentence   strange   weather`

→ highest list he read comfortably (about 8–10 right): **Grade ____**

## A5. Read these sentences out loud
1. `The big dog ran to the man.`
2. `She can see a red cat on the mat.`
3. `We went to the park to play with our friends.`
4. `The clever fox jumped over the broken fence.`

→ highest sentence he read smoothly: # ____   notes: ____________________

# PART B — MATH  (shorter — if reading is hard, **read the questions to him**; we're testing math here, not reading)

## B1. Counting
- Count out loud as high as he can: stopped at ____
- "How many?"  ● ● ● ● ● ● ●  → he says: ____  (answer: 7)

## B2. Which is more?
- `8` or `5`? ____    `13` or `31`? ____    `19` or `21`? ____

## B3. Read these numbers out loud
`7      15      40      108`  → which he got: ____________________

## B4. Tens and ones
- "In **34**, how many tens? how many ones?" → ____  (answer: 3 tens, 4 ones)

## B5. Add and subtract
`3 + 4 = ___`    `9 + 6 = ___`    `10 − 4 = ___`    `15 − 7 = ___`

→ how many of 4: ____   did he use fingers / count up / just know it: __________

## B6. One word problem (read it to him)
"You have **6** apples and get **5** more. How many now?" → ____  (answer: 11)
→ could he picture it / set it up? ____________________

# What we learn from this
You don't need to score it — just send the rough results back. Here's what they tell us:
- **Letter sounds + A2 shaky** → we start at the very foundation (sounds → blending). That's normal and very fixable with the right daily practice.
- **A2 fine, A3/A4 drop off** → he's an early reader; we build from where the words got hard.
- **Math:** B1–B4 show whether the *number sense under the symbols* is solid; B5–B6 show the next step. We start one notch **below** where he struggled, so he gets early wins.

# Two honest notes
1. **Reading first.** If reading is hard, a lot of the "math" trouble is really reading trouble (he can't read the problem). So we lead with reading — it lifts both.
2. **A big gap is worth a real screening.** If even Part A's early items are a struggle, that's useful information to bring to a **school psychologist or reading specialist** (often free through his school) — only a human can tell us *why*, and that's the one thing this practice can't do. We do the daily practice; they do the diagnosis. Both at once.
